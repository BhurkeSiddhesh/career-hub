My Notes

Opening line (say this verbatim first):

"I built a multi-agent AI decisioning system for a US grocery chain's refund operations. The problem wasn't just cost — it was that 10 reps were making 10 different decisions on the same case. I fixed that by building four AI agents that reason together and produce a consistent, explainable recommendation every time."

THE ACTUAL PROBLEM

Jewel-Osco (an Albertsons banner) had customer service reps manually approving or denying refund requests. No framework, no audit trail. Reps were inconsistent — same household, different outcomes depending on the rep handling the case. The business couldn't scale it and couldn't defend decisions to leadership.

WHY I STARTED WITH DATA, NOT THE MODEL

Before building any agent, I spent weeks creating three separate datasets — household level, order level, and item level. The hypothesis was that refund fraud risk lives in the combination of signals, not any single one. A high-value customer ordering a known problem SKU in an unusual pattern is a different risk profile than either signal alone. For instance, a household might be at the 95th percentile for spend, but suddenly ordering a high-fraud-risk item with an unusual velocity. This combinatorial risk is what heuristics miss. To capture this, I created 25+ KPIs across these three layers.

THE PERCENTILE TRICK — KEY ENGINEERING INSIGHT

Raw KPI values mean nothing to a reasoning model. A household spend of $3,400 over 6 months — is that high or low? I converted every KPI to a percentile score against the population. So the agent doesn't see "$3,400" — it sees "household spend = 94th percentile = top-tier customer in last 6 months." The model can now reason contextually: high-spend + legitimate SKU + standard order pattern = approve. New customer + high-value item + no order history = flag.

THE FOUR-AGENT STRUCTURE

Three specialist agents each analyzed the refund request from one lens — household agent (who is this customer?), order agent (what does this order look like?), item agent (what is the risk profile of this SKU?). Each produced a structured risk summary. A fourth decision agent read all three summaries and made the final call with written rationale. We used GPT-o3 specifically because we needed a reasoning model, not a generation model.

HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP — WHY IT WAS ESSENTIAL

Reps couldn't trust a system they couldn't interrogate. The PoC was designed with agent recommendation visible alongside reasoning — 'approve, because: household is 94th percentile spend, order matches typical pattern, item has 2% fraud rate in this region.' The rep validated or overrode. Our priority was to defend cases where reps were rejecting and our agent was accepting the refund request, and vice versa—that's where we had leverage to prove the model's reasoning. By showing the agent's logic side-by-side, reps could see exactly why a decision was made. The override rate started at ~40% in week one, but as reps began trusting the explanations, it dropped to under 15% by week six.

RESULTS: 500 cases, 5 stores — 11.3% refund cost reduction, 28% productivity savings, 89.16% decision consistency, 94.30% primary-driver reliability.

Q: Why o3 and why not something else?
We used GPT-o3 because this was fundamentally a reasoning problem, not a generation problem. We didn't need the model to write a fluent email; we needed it to synthesize three different risk summaries, weigh conflicting signals, and produce a logical, auditable decision. The explicit reasoning capabilities of o3 allowed it to adjudicate disagreements between the specialist agents much more reliably than standard instruction-tuned models.
Q: Why not just use heuristics instead of building an agentic system?
Heuristics work when decision rules are static and knowable upfront. The refund problem has neither property. The risk signal isn't in any single feature — it's in the interaction between features. A rule like 'if household spend is high, approve' would approve a high-spend customer actively gaming the system with a new fraud pattern. A rule like 'if SKU fraud rate is high, deny' would deny a genuine high-value customer returning a legitimately defective product. You'd need hundreds of combined rules to capture the interaction space, and the moment fraud patterns shift, every rule breaks and someone has to manually update them.

The agentic approach lets each specialist agent reason from its own context, and the decision agent reconciles them. When patterns shift, the reasoning model adapts without rule rewrites. We validated this on a holdout set with 3 novel fraud patterns not seen in training — heuristics would have missed all three, the agent system flagged two of them.
Q: What was your eval framework? How did you actually measure this?
Three distinct measurement layers.

DECISION CONSISTENCY (89.16%): We duplicated 200 cases with slight surface variations — same household data and risk signals, slightly rephrased customer messages. Ran both versions through the system and checked whether the final decision and primary driver matched. 89.16% match rate. The 10.84% inconsistency was mostly on genuinely borderline cases, which is expected and healthy.

PRIMARY-DRIVER RELIABILITY (94.30%): We had 3 senior reps manually label the most important risk factor for 100 cases. The agent system's stated primary driver matched the senior rep's label 94.3% of the time. This told us the system was reasoning on the right signals.

COST DELTA (11.3%): We simulated the full 500-case set with agent decisions vs. actual historical rep decisions. Mapped both to known fraud outcomes from chargeback data. The agent denied more true-positive fraud cases while maintaining approval rates for legitimate high-value customers.
Q: What challenges did you face?
DATA LINKAGE: Household, order, and item data lived in three separate systems with no shared unique key. We did probabilistic customer matching — linking loyalty IDs to transaction records to CRM profiles. About 12% of cases had incomplete linkage; we built a fallback where those got flagged for mandatory human review.

PERCENTILE CALIBRATION DRIFT: Percentiles computed on a static historical window go stale. A 90th percentile spender in January is a different dollar amount in December due to holiday volume. I built rolling 90-day windows, refreshed weekly.

AGENT DISAGREEMENT HANDLING: When all three specialist agents gave conflicting risk assessments, the decision agent had to adjudicate. We went through 11 prompt iterations before the adjudication logic felt consistent.

REP RESISTANCE: Some reps felt the system was being used to evaluate their past decisions. We addressed this by framing it explicitly as a support tool, not a performance tool.
Q: How would you have done this differently?
STRUCTURED OUTPUT SCHEMAS: Each agent produced semi-structured text summaries. I'd now force each specialist agent to output strict JSON — risk_score (0-100), top_3_signals with percentile values, confidence_level, recommended_action. Cleaner inputs make the decision agent more reliable and outputs more auditable.

SMALLER FINE-TUNED DECISION AGENT: GPT-o3 was expensive at scale. The specialist agents need full reasoning capability, but the decision agent is doing a more bounded task — synthesize three structured summaries into a binary decision with rationale. With 500+ labeled decisions, I'd fine-tune a smaller model on the decision layer.

EARLIER CO-DESIGN WITH REPS: I built the KPI framework from data analysis without enough rep input upfront. When we showed reps the system, they immediately identified two patterns I'd missed — 'same-item repeat return' and 'short-interval multi-SKU return.' Earlier co-design would have caught these before model build.
Q: What was success for you in this project?
The 11.3% cost number is what goes on the slide — but that wasn't what told me the project had worked.

The signal was the rep who was most skeptical at the start — she had been at Jewel-Osco for 9 years and was openly dismissive in the first two sessions. In week five, she used the agent's reasoning to explain her own decision to a supervisor who questioned her on a borderline approval. She said 'the system flagged it as low risk because of household spend and order history, and I agreed with that reasoning.' She had gone from resisting the tool to using it as professional backup.

The cost number is a consequence. The real achievement was making a system explainable enough that an experienced operator chose to own its reasoning as her own.
Q: What are the three specialist agents and what does each one do?
The three agents each reason from a different data layer:

EntityProfiler looks at who the customer is — household-level signals like total spend percentile, order count, days as a customer, loyalty history. It answers "is this a trustworthy customer?" It doesn't see raw dollar amounts — it sees "94th percentile spender over 6 months," which gives the model contextual meaning rather than a raw number.

ContextAnalysis looks at this specific order — order value relative to their history, timing, pattern vs. typical behaviour. It answers "does this order look normal for this customer?"

RiskSignal looks at the item being returned — historical fraud rate for that SKU or product category, return patterns for that product. It answers "is this a high-risk item?"

The fourth DecisionAgent reads all three structured summaries and makes the final call with written rationale. The separation is intentional — it makes the reasoning auditable and easy to debug when agents disagree.

Full Pitch
STAR
Hard Q&A
My Notes

Opening line:

"I solved a problem that makes most segmentation projects fail in production — cluster instability across time. Brenntag needed monthly SKU segments that planners could act on repeatably. Standard clustering gives you different shapes every month. I built a rolling-profile approach that makes clusters stable enough to attach business playbooks to."

THE REAL PROBLEM (not the one in the brief)

The stated ask was 'segment our SKUs.' The actual problem was that the business had tried this before and it never stuck — because whoever had done it before computed clusters on point-in-time data. Clustering on September data gives you five beautiful clusters in September. Run it again in October and you get five completely different clusters with different members. A planner cannot build a playbook on a cluster that reshuffles every month.

WHY THIS WAS HARD

Chemical distribution SKUs have high demand variability — volumes spike for industrial procurement cycles, crash during plant shutdowns, recover. Monthly data is noisy. The naive fix (moving average smoothing) loses the trend signal you need to distinguish genuinely declining SKUs from cyclically slow ones.

MY SOLUTION — ROLLING 12-MONTH BEHAVIORAL PROFILES

Instead of clustering on point-in-time KPIs, I computed each SKU's behavioral fingerprint across trailing 12 months: average monthly volume, coefficient of variation (demand stability), order frequency, margin contribution, and trend slope. These describe the SKU's character, not its state. A SKU that is highly variable, low-frequency, and declining has a consistent fingerprint even if its actual September volume bounces around. Applied PCA to reduce multicollinearity, then K-means on the stable profile space.

WHY K=5

Two reasons. The business already had five planning playbooks — fast mover, standard, slow mover, critical low-stock, discontinue review. I started from that constraint and validated with silhouette scores that five was also mathematically defensible (silhouette = 0.61 vs 0.48 for K=4 and 0.57 for K=6).

OPERATIONAL DELIVERY

Automated monthly pipeline — compute rolling KPIs on the 1st of each month, run PCA + K-means, map clusters to business actions via heuristic thresholds, export to AWS S3 and Power BI. Planners get their segment assignments every month without touching the model.

SituationBrenntag needed reliable monthly SKU segmentation across 4 EU plants — previous attempts failed because cluster membership reshuffled every month, making business playbooks impossible to attach.
TaskBuild a segmentation engine where clusters are stable enough month-over-month that planners can apply consistent actions to each segment.
ActionComputed 12-month rolling behavioral profiles per SKU rather than point-in-time KPIs, applied PCA + K-means (K=5), mapped clusters to 5 business actions via heuristic thresholds, automated full pipeline to AWS S3 + Power BI.
ResultAutomated monthly segmentation replacing 3 days of manual planner work. Cluster stability (Adjusted Rand Index) of 0.81 month-over-month. 88% planner agreement on cluster assignments in validation.
Q: Why did you not build basic heuristic based segmentation instead of modelling?
Basic heuristics like "if volume > X and frequency > Y then Fast Mover" break down across a diverse network of plants. A threshold that works for a high-throughput facility will misclassify everything at a smaller facility. Furthermore, heuristics struggle with trend detection and noise—they are brittle to the high demand variability in chemical distribution. By using unsupervised learning on 12-month behavioral profiles, the model discovers the natural structure of the data automatically, adapting to different plant contexts without needing manual rule recalibration.
Q: Why not build a classification model with fixed thresholds per plant and reuse it across months?
A classification model would work if you can define upfront what 'high priority' means for every plant — and if that definition stays stable. For a single plant with experienced planners who understand their SKU universe, a threshold-based classifier is simpler and more interpretable.

The problem is scale and knowledge transfer. Brenntag has ~2,000 plants globally, not 4. A classification model requires labeled training data — which means someone has to define 'high priority' for each plant's specific KPI distributions. You'd need local planner input for 2,000 plants, ongoing label maintenance, and retraining whenever a plant's product mix changes. That is not scalable.

Clustering discovers natural groupings from data without requiring pre-labeled ground truth. The business action mapping is done once at the global level and interpreted locally. That said, a hybrid approach is valid: run clustering once to discover natural groupings, then train a lightweight classifier on those cluster labels to score new SKUs in real-time.
Q: How could you have made the clusters more actionable?
This is the honest gap in what I built. K-means optimizes for within-cluster variance — it finds compact, well-separated groups mathematically. It does not guarantee those groups are maximally useful for business decisions.

CONSTRAINED CLUSTERING: Add business constraints as penalties in the optimization. For example: 'no cluster should have fewer than 50 SKUs' (too small to build a playbook), 'each cluster must have a distinct action implication.' Libraries like k-means-constrained support minimum/maximum cluster size constraints.

CO-DESIGN UPFRONT: I built the KPI set from data analysis, then validated with planners after the fact. The planners identified that 'order frequency' mattered more than I had weighted it — a SKU ordered once a year in large volume is operationally very different from one ordered weekly in small quantities, even if total annual volume is identical.

INTERPRETABILITY LAYER: Each cluster got a numeric label. I would add an auto-naming layer — given each cluster's centroid profile, generate a plain-language descriptor: 'high-velocity, stable demand, premium margin' vs 'low-frequency, high-variability, declining trend.' Planners adopt segments faster when they have intuitive names.
Q: How did you measure whether this actually worked?
This was a PoC so we did not have long-term inventory impact data. Here is what we actually measured:

CLUSTER STABILITY (primary metric): Adjusted Rand Index between Month N and Month N-1 cluster assignments. ARI = 1 is perfect stability, 0 is random. We targeted ARI > 0.75 and achieved 0.81 across the three-month test window. The 0.19 instability was concentrated in mid-tier SKUs near cluster boundaries — expected and acceptable.

PLANNER VALIDATION: Reviewed 50 cluster assignments with planning leads. Asked: 'Does this cluster assignment match your intuition for this SKU?' 88% agreement. The 12% disagreements were mostly SKUs undergoing a genuine behavioral transition where the 12-month rolling window was slow to reflect the change — pointing to a needed improvement: shorter rolling windows for trend-detection alongside longer windows for stability scoring.

PROCESS VALUE: Manual segmentation took 3 planner-days per month across the 4 plants. Automated pipeline ran overnight.
Q: What is Adjusted Rand Index and why did you use it?
ARI measures how similar two clusterings are to each other. A score of 1.0 means cluster membership is identical between the two runs. A score of 0 means the overlap is no better than random.

I used it specifically to measure month-over-month cluster stability — did the same SKUs end up in the same segments in October as they did in September? We achieved 0.81, meaning the vast majority of SKUs stayed in the same cluster across consecutive months.

This matters because Brenntag's previous segmentation attempts failed in production — not because the clusters were mathematically wrong, but because they reshuffled every month and planners couldn't build repeatable playbooks on moving targets. ARI gave us a single number to prove that problem was solved. We targeted above 0.75 as the minimum acceptable threshold and hit 0.81.

Full Pitch

Hard Q&A
My Notes

Opening line:

"Fashion demand forecasting is one of the hardest forecasting problems because the signal you most need — trend — is exactly what breaks standard models. I built a hybrid system that combined Prophet's trend decomposition with Gradient Boosting's feature learning, and layered in SKU behavioral clusters to give the model 'this shoe is seasonal' as structured context rather than hoping it figures that out from raw data."

WHY FASHION IS HARD

Most forecasting literature is built on FMCG — stable products, long history, predictable seasonality. Fashion footwear has short product lifecycles (many SKUs live 1–2 seasons), trend-driven demand spikes that don't recur, and high cannibalization between new and old styles. You cannot extrapolate last year's sales of a boot that has been discontinued.

THE CLUSTER INPUT — KEY ARCHITECTURAL CHOICE

I ran a separate clustering exercise on demand shape patterns — not demand level, but demand shape. How does this SKU's sales curve look across a year? Found 6 shapes: monsoon peak, festive spike, steady-state formal, athleisure year-round, summer sandal, winter boot. Each SKU was assigned a cluster label based on its historical demand shape. This label went into the forecasting model as a categorical feature — telling the model 'this SKU follows the monsoon pattern' as structured input.

THE PROPHET HYBRID

Prophet is good at one specific thing — decomposing a time series into trend + seasonality + holiday effects. It is not a great direct forecasting model for erratic data. But its trend and seasonality decomposition components are genuinely useful signals. I ran Prophet on each SKU's historical data, extracted its forward-projected trend and seasonality components as input features to the Gradient Boosting model. GBM then learned which Prophet signals were reliable for which cluster types.

M+2 HORIZON AND PHASED DEPLOYMENT

We forecast the 3rd month from today (M+2). Top 200 SKUs (80%+ of revenue) hit MAPE under 20% — the operational threshold for automated reorder. We shipped those first. The remaining ~800 SKUs had higher error — rather than deploy bad forecasts, we kept those on planner judgment with the model output as a reference signal.

REORDER POINT CALCULATION

Reorder point = forecast demand over lead time + safety stock buffer. Safety stock was calculated from historical demand variability (standard deviation of weekly demand x z-score for desired service level). The model output directly fed this formula — closing the loop from forecast to action.

SituationMetro Brands had 15%+ stockout rate on popular styles and overstock on slow movers — inaccurate demand forecasts across 320+ stores and 1,000 SKUs.
TaskBuild a demand forecast accurate enough to drive automated reorder point calculations for fashion footwear, where erratic trends break standard models.
ActionCreated 6 demand-shape clusters as model features, used Prophet decomposition outputs as hybrid inputs to Gradient Boosting, forecast M+2 horizon, phased deployment starting with top 200 SKUs meeting MAPE <20%.
Result15% improvement in overall forecast accuracy. Top 200 SKUs at MAPE <20% deployed to automated reorder. Client reported ~12% stockout reduction and ~8% dead stock reduction 8 weeks post-deployment.
Q: What did you actually try for the remaining 800 SKUs and why didn't it work?
I want to be direct here because this is a real limitation I could not overcome.

Thing 1: Added more features — price elasticity, promotional flags, store-region variables, nearby competitor pricing. Marginal improvement, roughly 1-2 MAPE points. The fundamental problem is data sparsity: many SKUs had 12-18 months of monthly data, which is 12-18 training observations. No feature engineering fixes that.

Thing 2: Global model — a single GBM trained across all 1,000 SKUs simultaneously, with SKU cluster embeddings as identifiers. The idea was to transfer learning from data-rich SKUs to data-poor ones. This helped mid-tier SKUs (MAPE dropped ~3 points for ~200 SKUs in the 300-500 rank band) but barely moved the long tail. The demand patterns for truly sparse SKUs were too idiosyncratic to learn from analogous SKUs.

Thing 3: Intermittent demand models — Croston's method, designed for SKUs with frequent zero-demand periods. Mixed results. Helped for genuinely intermittent SKUs but not for erratic-but-not-zero patterns.

Honest answer: for new fashion SKUs with limited history, the best approach is analogical reasoning — find historically similar SKUs at the same lifecycle stage and use their trajectory. I did not have time to build that, and it is what I would tackle first in a second phase.
Q: How did you define success for this project?
Two explicit success criteria agreed upfront, one emergent.

MODEL CRITERION: MAPE < 20% for top 200 SKUs. This threshold came from a conversation with the planning team — below 20% MAPE, the expected cost of forecast-driven inventory errors was lower than the cost of manual planning hours. Above it, human judgment was cheaper. We hit this for 82% of the top 200 SKUs.

BUSINESS CRITERION: Reduction in stockout and overstock events 8 weeks post-deployment. Client reported ~12% reduction in stockout events for forecast-driven SKUs and ~8% reduction in dead stock value. I want to be careful attributing all of this to the model — there were other supply chain changes happening simultaneously. But the direction was correct.

EMERGENT SIGNAL: The planning team started using the forecast error distribution — not just the point forecast — to set safety stock buffers. High-uncertainty SKUs got larger buffers. This was not in the original scope; they derived it themselves from model outputs. That kind of adoption beyond the designed use case is the best signal that a model has been genuinely integrated into decision-making.

SND Simulation (Supply Network Design)

Network Optimization / Simulation for P&G, Japan. Feb 2024 – Oct 2024.

Client P&G, Japan
Type Network Optimization / Simulation

Full Pitch
STAR
Hard Q&A
My Notes

Opening line:

"I built a supply network simulation for P&G Japan that found $1M in logistics savings — not by changing routes or carriers, but by identifying that the company had a commercial lever it was not using: flexible delivery date windows in customer contracts that had never been systematically exploited for consolidation."

THE PROBLEM FRAMING

P&G Japan was dispatching partial truckloads because orders were scheduled to rigid Required Delivery Dates. The insight was that some customer contracts had RDD flexibility — plus or minus 1 or 2 days — that the logistics team never used because there was no tool to identify which orders could be pulled forward without violating the customer commitment.

HOW THE SIMULATION WORKED

Three components. First, a flexibility filter — parse every open future order against its contract RDD tolerance to identify consolidation candidates. Only orders with documented RDD flexibility were included. This conservative filter excluded ~60% of order volume but made the output defensible to procurement and commercial teams. Second, a consolidation engine — for each consolidation-eligible order, model whether pulling it to today's shipment would bring the truck above 85% fill rate (threshold for full-truck economics). Third, a cost model — calculate the unit cost delta between consolidated and unconsolidated dispatch, multiplied by order volume, summed by customer.

WHY TOP 10 CUSTOMERS = $1M

In B2B logistics, the Pareto principle is extreme — top 10 customers typically represent 70-80% of volume. In P&G Japan's case, these customers also had the most flexible RDD contracts (negotiated over long commercial relationships). The $1M was heavily concentrated in 3 of the top 10 customers where contract flexibility was highest and current fill rates were lowest.

OPERATIONAL OUTPUT

The simulation ran daily. Each morning, planners saw a ranked list: 'these 8 orders should be held until tomorrow's dispatch to form a full truck for Customer X, saving ¥XXX per order.' It was a decision tool, not a reporting tool.

SituationP&G Japan was dispatching partial truckloads on fixed RDD schedules — leaving truck capacity underutilized because no system identified when consolidation was contractually possible.
TaskBuild a simulation that identifies order consolidation opportunities using RDD flexibility windows, quantifies savings per customer, and gives planners daily actionable recommendations.
ActionBuilt a three-layer simulation: RDD flexibility filter from contract data, consolidation engine modeling fill-rate impact of pulling orders forward, cost model calculating savings per consolidation action. Output as daily planner decision tool.
Result$1M savings opportunity identified across top 10 customers. Piloted with highest-opportunity customer — actual savings matched model prediction within 8% variance over 4-week pilot.
Q: How did you actually model the truck fill calculation — what were the constraints?
Truck capacity is multi-dimensional — weight, volume, and for some P&G categories, hazmat classification compatibility. We simplified to weight-based fill for the initial model, which was the binding constraint for ~70% of routes. Volume constraints were relevant for bulkier SKUs (detergent, fabric care) but we flagged those for manual review rather than auto-including them in the consolidation recommendation.

The 85% fill threshold was not arbitrary — it came from P&G's own carrier contracts, which had a rate break at 85% fill. Below that, spot rates apply. Above it, contracted rates apply. The $1M calculation was based on the contracted vs. spot rate differential multiplied by the consolidatable order volume.
Q: How did you validate the $1M figure was not just theoretical?
Three validation steps.

First, we ran the model on 3 months of historical order data where actual dispatch outcomes were known — the model correctly identified consolidation opportunities that planners had manually executed in 76% of cases. The 24% miss rate was mostly due to late-breaking order changes that the model would not have seen at the daily run time.

Second, the $1M was conservative — we only included orders with documented RDD flexibility, which was 40% of order volume. The real opportunity is probably 1.5-2x larger, but unverified flexibility creates commercial risk.

Third, we piloted with one customer for 4 weeks. Actual savings matched the model's prediction within 8% variance — primarily because one large order in week 3 had an unusual weight profile the model had slightly miscalculated.
Q: What were the data challenges in this project?
The RDD flexibility data was the hardest part. It lived in customer contract documents — PDFs and Excel attachments in an email archive, not in the order management system. I had to work with the commercial team to extract flexibility windows for each customer account and build a reference table. This took 3 weeks and was the project's longest single task.

The second challenge was order change frequency. P&G Japan had an order amendment rate of about 18% within the 48-hour window before dispatch. An order that is a consolidation candidate at 8am might have its quantity or timing amended by 3pm. The model re-ran twice daily to account for this. In production, this would need a webhook-triggered recalculation whenever an order amendment came in.
Q: Walk me through the three layers of the simulation in detail.
Layer 1 —

RDD Flexibility Filter

: RDD means Required Delivery Date. Some P&G customer contracts allowed delivery 1-2 days early or late. This layer parsed every open future order against its contract terms and flagged only the ones with documented flexibility. About 60% of order volume was excluded here — those orders were locked to a specific date and couldn't be touched. This conservative filter made the output commercially defensible.

Layer 2 —

Fill-Rate Engine

: For each flexible order, the engine asked: if we pull this order forward to today's truck, does that truck now hit 85% capacity? The 85% threshold wasn't arbitrary — it came from P&G's own carrier contracts, which had a rate break at that fill level. Below 85%, spot rates apply. Above it, contracted rates apply. So the engine was specifically modeling whether consolidation would trigger a cost-tier change.

Layer 3 —

Cost Model

: For every consolidation the engine approved, it calculated the actual yen saving — the delta between spot and contracted rate, multiplied by order weight, multiplied by dispatch frequency. Summed across all consolidatable orders for the top 10 customers, this produced the $1M figure. The $1M was conservative because it only included the 40% of orders with verified RDD flexibility.

"I built a shipment consolidation recommendation system for Schneider Electric's North American logistics. Q: How does Markov chain attribution actually work?
The core idea: model each user's journey from first SEO touch to becoming active as a sequence of states (touchpoints). Use historical journey data to estimate transition probabilities between states — what is the probability that a user who landed on a category page goes next to a product page vs. bounces?

Then, for each touchpoint, calculate the removal effect: if you delete all journeys containing this touchpoint and re-run the model, what % of conversions disappear? A touchpoint with a 35% removal effect is responsible for 35% of conversions. Sum removal effects across touchpoints, normalize to 100%, and you have attribution weights.

Why this over simpler multi-touch models? Linear says every touch is equal. Time-decay says recent touches matter more. These are arbitrary mathematical assumptions. Markov chain derives attribution from actual observed journey patterns — it is empirical, not assumed. For a company with massive journey data, the empirical approach is meaningfully more accurate.

Q: What is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) and why is it practically important in analytics?
CLT states that the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as the sample size gets larger, regardless of the population's underlying distribution (assuming finite variance). Practically, it allows us to use normal-distribution-based tests (like t-tests or z-tests) to compare sample means (e.g., in A/B testing) even if the underlying data (like revenue per user or click-through rates) is highly skewed or non-normal.
Q: How do you explain a p-value to a non-technical stakeholder, and what is its biggest misconception?
To a stakeholder: "Assuming our new feature actually has zero effect, the p-value is the probability that we would see a result this positive just by random luck." The biggest misconception is that the p-value is the probability that the null hypothesis is true. A p-value of 0.03 doesn't mean there's a 3% chance the result is a fluke; it means if the feature does nothing, we'd see this data 3% of the time.
Q: What is the difference between a t-test and ANOVA, and when do you use each?
A t-test compares the means of exactly two groups (e.g., Control vs. Variant). ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) compares the means of three or more groups. You use ANOVA instead of multiple t-tests to control the Family-Wise Error Rate (Type I error). If you ran three separate t-tests for groups A, B, and C, your chance of a false positive inflates beyond your alpha (e.g. 0.05).
Q: What do R-squared and Adjusted R-squared measure, and why is Adjusted R-squared preferred in multiple regression?
R-squared measures the proportion of variance in the dependent variable explained by the independent variables. The problem with R-squared is that it monotonically increases as you add more variables, even if they are noise. Adjusted R-squared penalizes the addition of non-informative variables, only increasing if the new variable improves the model more than would be expected by chance. It prevents overfitting in linear models.
Q: What is the difference between Type I and Type II errors, and how do you prioritize them?
Type I error is a False Positive (rejecting a true null hypothesis, e.g., concluding a feature works when it doesn't). Type II error is a False Negative (failing to reject a false null hypothesis, e.g., missing a real effect). Priority depends on the business context. For medical diagnosis or fraud detection, minimizing Type II is critical. For UX changes where development cost is high, minimizing Type I is preferred.
Q: What are Degrees of Freedom in statistics?
Degrees of freedom (DF) represent the number of independent values or quantities that can vary in an analysis without breaking any constraints. In a simple variance calculation of a sample (N), DF is N-1 because once the sample mean is known, only N-1 values are free to vary. DF relates directly to the power of statistical tests and model complexity.
Q: Explain Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) and its relation to regression.
MLE is a method for estimating the parameters of a statistical model by maximizing the likelihood function, making the observed data most probable. In Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) linear regression, assuming normally distributed errors, minimizing the mean squared error (MSE) is mathematically equivalent to performing MLE to find the parameters.
Q: What is heteroscedasticity and why is it a problem in linear regression?
Heteroscedasticity occurs when the variance of the residuals is not constant across all levels of the independent variables (e.g., error grows as the predicted value grows). It violates a key assumption of OLS, meaning the standard errors are biased. This makes hypothesis tests (p-values, confidence intervals) unreliable, though the coefficient estimates themselves remain unbiased.
Q: How do you detect and handle multicollinearity?
Multicollinearity happens when independent variables are highly correlated with each other. It inflates the variance of coefficient estimates, making them unstable and hard to interpret. It's detected using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). If VIF > 5-10, you can handle it by dropping correlated features, combining them, or using regularization (like Ridge/Lasso) which is robust to multicollinearity.
Q: What factors affect the Statistical Power of a test?
Statistical power is the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis (1 - Type II error rate). It is affected by four main factors: Sample size (larger = more power), Effect size (larger = more power), Significance level / Alpha (higher alpha = more power), and Variance (lower variance = more power).
Q: How do Gradient Boosting and Random Forest differ fundamentally?
Both are ensemble methods using decision trees, but they combine them differently. Random Forest builds independent trees in parallel using bagging (bootstrap aggregating) and random feature selection, reducing variance. Gradient Boosting builds trees sequentially, where each new tree tries to correct the residual errors of the previous ensemble, reducing bias.
Q: What are the key differences between XGBoost and CatBoost, and when would you choose one over the other?
XGBoost is a highly optimized gradient boosting framework using pre-sorting and histogram-based splits. CatBoost (Categorical Boosting) is specifically designed to handle categorical variables natively using target-based statistics (ordered target encoding) without requiring one-hot encoding. For a dataset with many high-cardinality categoricals, CatBoost is generally faster to develop and often more robust. XGBoost might be preferred for purely numerical data or when extreme hyperparameter tuning is required.
Q: Why do we need Cross Validation, and when does standard K-Fold fail?
Cross-validation provides a robust estimate of out-of-sample model performance by training and evaluating on multiple distinct splits of the data. Standard K-fold randomly assigns rows to folds. It fails in time-series data (because you cannot train on the future to predict the past—requires TimeSeriesSplit), or when there are grouped dependencies (e.g., multiple rows for the same customer—requires GroupKFold to prevent data leakage).
Q: How do you detect and address overfitting vs underfitting in a model building lifecycle?
Underfitting (high bias) means the model is too simple; both train and validation scores are poor. Address it by increasing tree depth, adding features, or decreasing learning rate while increasing the number of trees. Overfitting (high variance) means the train score is great but the validation score drops. Address it by decreasing max depth, increasing L1/L2 regularization, increasing minimum child weight, or lowering the learning rate and using early stopping.
Q: Explain the Bias-Variance Tradeoff and how it maps to hyperparameter tuning.
Bias is error from erroneous assumptions (underfitting), while variance is error from sensitivity to small fluctuations in training data (overfitting). As you increase model complexity (e.g., deeper trees, lower regularization), bias decreases and variance increases. Tuning hyperparameters is the process of finding the 'sweet spot' that minimizes total error on unseen data.
Q: What is the difference between L1 (Lasso) and L2 (Ridge) Regularization? Why does L1 cause sparsity?
Both add a penalty term to the loss function to prevent overfitting. L2 adds squared magnitude of coefficients, shrinking them evenly but rarely to exactly zero. L1 adds the absolute magnitude. Because the derivative of the L1 penalty is constant, it pushes coefficients all the way to zero, effectively performing feature selection and creating sparse models.
Q: What strategies do you use for handling severely imbalanced data?
Techniques include: 1) Changing the evaluation metric to PR-AUC or F1. 2) Using class weights to penalize errors on the minority class more heavily. 3) Resampling (SMOTE to synthesize minority cases, or downsampling the majority class). Generally, algorithms with class weights (like XGBoost's scale_pos_weight) are preferred over synthetic resampling, which can distort the feature space.
Q: When does K-Means fail and when would you use DBSCAN instead?
K-Means assumes clusters are spherical and similarly sized, and it struggles with outliers because it minimizes variance. It fails on complex, non-linear cluster shapes (like concentric circles). DBSCAN groups based on density. It doesn't require specifying 'K', handles noise/outliers naturally, and can find arbitrarily shaped clusters, making it vastly superior for spatial data.
Q: How does PCA (Principal Component Analysis) work and what are its limitations?
PCA is a dimensionality reduction technique that transforms data into orthogonal components that capture the maximum variance. It works via eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix. Limitations: It assumes linear relationships between features, scaling is strictly required, and the resulting principal components lack interpretability compared to original business features.
Q: How are feature importances calculated in tree models, and what are the flaws?
By default, tree models use Gini impurity decrease (or variance reduction) across all splits using a feature. The major flaw is that Gini importance is heavily biased towards high-cardinality continuous features. Permutation importance (shuffling a feature and measuring the drop in performance) or SHAP values are much more reliable, model-agnostic methods.
Q: Explain the "Curse of Dimensionality."
As the number of features (dimensions) increases, the volume of the feature space increases exponentially, making the data sparse. This causes distance-based metrics (like Euclidean distance in KNN or K-Means) to lose meaning, because the distance between any two points becomes roughly similar, degrading algorithmic performance.
Q: What are SHAP values and how do they explain predictions?
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) uses game theory to assign each feature an importance value for a specific prediction. Unlike global feature importance, SHAP explains *local* predictions (e.g., exactly why Customer A was denied a loan) by showing how each feature pushes the model output from the base expected value to the final prediction.
Q: Explain the components of a Confusion Matrix and how they translate to business costs.
It breaks down predictions into True Positives (TP), True Negatives (TN), False Positives (FP), and False Negatives (FN). In an analytics context, these map directly to business costs. For example, in fraud detection, an FP is declining a good customer (costing LTV and trust), while an FN is letting a fraudulent transaction through (costing the transaction value).
Q: How do you decide whether to optimize for Precision or Recall?
Precision is TP / (TP + FP) — "Of all the cases I flagged, how many were actually true?" Recall is TP / (TP + FN) — "Of all the actual true cases, how many did I find?" You optimize for Precision when the cost of a False Positive is very high (e.g., spam filter, recommending a drastic medical treatment). You optimize for Recall when the cost of a False Negative is high (e.g., cancer screening, high-risk fraud detection).
Q: When is F1 Score a better metric than Accuracy?
Accuracy fails on imbalanced datasets. If only 1% of transactions are fraud, a model that predicts "No Fraud" 100% of the time has 99% accuracy but is completely useless. F1 Score is the harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. It is a much better metric when you have class imbalance and care about the positive class, because it requires both Precision and Recall to be reasonable to get a high score.
Q: When should you use the Precision-Recall (PR) AUC instead of ROC AUC?
ROC AUC plots True Positive Rate vs False Positive Rate across all thresholds. PR AUC plots Precision vs Recall. ROC AUC can be overly optimistic when dealing with highly imbalanced datasets, because a large number of True Negatives keeps the FPR low even if you have many False Positives. If the positive class is rare and you care mostly about finding it without drowning in false alarms, PR AUC is a much more informative metric.
Q: Why use Log Loss (Cross-Entropy Loss) instead of accuracy for evaluating probabilities?
Accuracy evaluates hard class assignments, ignoring confidence. A model predicting 0.51 and one predicting 0.99 for a positive class both get the same accuracy if the threshold is 0.5. Log loss penalizes the model based on the distance between the predicted probability and the true label, severely punishing confident wrong answers. It forces the model to be well-calibrated.
Q: How are ROC AUC and the Gini Coefficient related?
In binary classification, the Gini coefficient (commonly used in credit scoring) is a linear transformation of the ROC AUC. The formula is: Gini = 2 * AUC - 1. An AUC of 0.75 means a Gini of 0.50. Both measure the model's ability to rank order predictions correctly regardless of threshold.
Q: When do you use MAPE vs RMSE for regression evaluation?
RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) heavily penalizes large errors because of the square, and it is in the absolute units of the target. MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) evaluates error relative to the actual value, providing a percentage that's easy for business stakeholders to understand. However, MAPE fails when actual values are zero or close to zero.
Q: How do you evaluate the quality of unsupervised clustering?
Without ground truth labels, we use intrinsic metrics like the Silhouette Score. It measures how similar an object is to its own cluster (cohesion) compared to other clusters (separation). Values range from -1 to 1; a high value indicates the object is well matched to its own cluster and poorly matched to neighboring clusters.
Q: When is a simple Holdout set preferred over K-Fold Cross-Validation?
K-Fold is computationally expensive. If you are dealing with massive datasets (e.g., millions of rows) and deep learning models that take days to train, a single large holdout (train/val/test split) is preferred because the variance of the performance estimate is already low due to the large sample size.
Q: What is a Calibration Curve and why is model calibration important?
A calibration curve plots predicted probabilities against actual observed frequencies. Calibration is vital for business decisions. If a model predicts a 20% risk of default, the business needs exactly 20 out of 100 such people to default to calculate expected values correctly. Tree models (like Random Forest) push probabilities away from 0 and 1, often requiring Platt Scaling or Isotonic Regression to recalibrate.
Q: How do you perform multiple aggregations on different columns simultaneously in pandas using groupby?
You pass a dictionary to the .agg() function. For example: df.groupby('customer_id').agg({'purchase_amt': ['sum', 'mean'], 'order_id': 'count'}). This allows you to define exactly which operations happen on which columns without running multiple groupby statements.
Q: Why and when do you use reset_index() after a groupby?
When you use groupby(), the grouping columns become the index of the resulting DataFrame. Using reset_index() moves those index levels back into standard columns. This is critical when you want to merge the aggregated data back with another dataset or use those columns as features in a machine learning model, as most libraries expect standard columns, not indices.
Q: What is the difference between melt() and pivot(), and when do you use melt?
melt() is used to unpivot a DataFrame from wide format to long format, whereas pivot() does the reverse. You use melt when your dataset has values spread across multiple columns (e.g., Jan_Sales, Feb_Sales, Mar_Sales) and you need them in a normalized long format (e.g., columns Month and Sales) which is required for seaborn plotting or grouped modeling.
Q: How are diff() and pct_change() used in data transformation?
.diff() calculates the absolute difference between the current row and a previous row (usually periods=1). .pct_change() calculates the percentage difference. These are essential for making time-series data stationary (removing trends) before feeding them into models, or for generating velocity features (e.g., "change in spend from last month") for behavioral models.
Q: Why is vectorization faster than .apply() or loops in pandas?
Loops in Python are slow due to interpretation overhead and type-checking at every iteration. Vectorized operations in pandas delegate the loop to highly optimized C-level code and take advantage of SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) processor instructions, performing the operation on entire arrays simultaneously, resulting in orders of magnitude speedups.
Q: Explain the differences between merge, join, and concat in pandas.
concat stacks DataFrames either vertically (rows) or horizontally (columns), aligning purely on the index. merge is equivalent to a SQL JOIN, allowing complex combinations based on specific columns (keys) rather than the index. join is a convenience method built on top of merge that explicitly joins two DataFrames on their indices.
Q: What are advanced strategies for handling missing data beyond mean/median imputation?
For time-series, forward-fill (ffill) or interpolation (linear/spline) maintains sequential logic. For cross-sectional data, iterative imputation (like MICE) models each feature as a function of the others. Another strategy is to add an "is_missing" boolean indicator column and fill the missing numeric with an extreme value (like -999) so tree models can explicitly learn the pattern of "missingness".
Q: What are lambda functions and when are they useful in pandas?
Lambda functions are anonymous, inline functions (e.g., lambda x: x*2). In pandas, they are heavily used inside .apply(), .assign(), or aggregation pipelines for custom, quick transformations without needing to define a formal def function block, keeping data wrangling code concise and chained.
Q: How do you use Rolling Windows in time-series feature engineering?
The .rolling(window=N) method creates a moving window over the data. By applying aggregations like .mean(), .std(), or .max(), you can engineer features like "7-day average spend" or "30-day volatility", which are highly predictive in forecasting and behavioral models because they capture momentum and recent context.
Q: How do you perform Window/Analytic functions (like SQL Rank) in pandas?
You combine groupby() with transformation methods. For instance, to rank customers by spend within each country: df.groupby('country')['spend'].rank(method='dense', ascending=False). To get running totals: df.groupby('customer')['spend'].cumsum(). To get the previous row's value: .shift(1).
Q: How does Bayes' Theorem apply to real-world business problems?
Bayes' Theorem updates our belief in a hypothesis based on new evidence. In business, it explains the "Base Rate Fallacy." For instance, if a rare disease (1 in 10,000) has a test that is 99% accurate, a positive test still means the person likely does not have the disease, because the base rate is so low. In analytics, this means even a highly accurate model predicting a rare event (like clicking an ad) will yield mostly false positives.
Q: How do you use Expected Value for decision making?
Expected value is the probability-weighted sum of all possible outcomes. EV = Σ (Probability * Value). It's the foundation of decision theory. If an ML model predicts a 10% chance of conversion on a $100 item, the EV of a marketing touch is $10. If the marketing touch costs $15, you shouldn't do it, even if the model's accuracy is perfect.
Q: What is the difference between Independent and Mutually Exclusive events?
Mutually exclusive events cannot happen at the same time (e.g., a customer cannot churn and renew on the same day). P(A and B) = 0. Independent events mean the outcome of one does not affect the outcome of the other (e.g., Customer A churning does not affect Customer B churning). P(A and B) = P(A) * P(B). Confusing these is a common error in risk modeling.
Q: Why is the Law of Large Numbers crucial for analytics?
It states that as the number of trials increases, the sample mean will converge to the expected value. This is why small sample sizes in A/B testing are dangerous—early results are highly volatile and prone to extreme values. You must wait for the sample size to be sufficiently large for the metric to stabilize to its true population parameter.
Q: When do you use the Poisson Distribution?
The Poisson distribution models the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, given a known constant mean rate of occurrence (lambda), and assuming events are independent. It is widely used for modeling queuing systems, like the number of customer support calls per hour, or the number of website clicks per minute.
Q: What is the difference between Binomial and Hypergeometric distributions?
Both model the number of successes in a set of trials. The Binomial assumes sampling *with* replacement, meaning the probability of success remains constant across trials (independent). The Hypergeometric models sampling *without* replacement from a finite population, meaning each draw changes the probability for the next draw (dependent).
Q: Explain Markov Chains and the "memoryless property."
A Markov Chain is a stochastic model describing a sequence of possible events. The defining "memoryless property" (Markov property) states that the probability of transitioning to the next state depends *only* on the current state, not on the sequence of events that preceded it. This is used in attribution modeling (e.g., Markov Chain attribution) to evaluate customer journey touchpoints.
Q: Provide a business example of Simpson's Paradox.
Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend appears in different groups of data but disappears or reverses when the groups are combined. Example: An A/B test shows Variant B increases conversion rates overall. But when analyzing Mobile and Desktop users separately, Variant A is better for *both*. This happens due to confounding variables (e.g., Variant B accidentally got way more Desktop traffic, which inherently converts higher).
Q: How is Survival Analysis (like Kaplan-Meier) used for customer churn?
Standard classification predicts *if* a customer will churn. Survival analysis predicts *when* they will churn (time-to-event). The Kaplan-Meier estimator calculates the probability of surviving past a certain time point. This handles "censored" data (customers who haven't churned yet) correctly, allowing businesses to calculate Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) curves dynamically.
Q: What is the difference between Combinatorics and Permutations?
Combinations count the number of ways to choose items from a set where the order *does not* matter (e.g., picking a committee of 3 people from 10). Permutations count the number of ways to arrange items where the order *does* matter (e.g., picking a President, VP, and Secretary from 10 people). In business, combinations are used for A/B multivariate test scoping.
